Information Literacy Session

Duration: 50 Minutes | Audience: First-Year Community College Students
WCAG 2.1 AA Compliant Lesson

Facilitator Lesson Plan

1. Opening Hook (5 Minutes)

Activity: Live Polling (Active Learning)

  • Action: Display a visual of a highly shared social media post making a bold, fake scientific claim. Provide an audio description of the image for the class. [Alt-text for digital display: A screenshot of a fake social media post claiming that eating a specific type of rock cures the common cold, with thousands of likes and shares.]
  • Prompt: Use a digital polling tool (or raise hands/colored index cards for a low-tech, accessible alternative) to ask: "Would you use this as a source for a college research paper? Yes, No, or Not Sure?"
  • Connection: Explain that navigating today’s information landscape is tough, but today they will learn the tools to confidently evaluate sources and share their findings accessibly.

2. Direct Instruction & Guided Discovery (10 Minutes)

  • Databases & Boolean: Introduce Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT) by comparing them to online shopping. If you search for "shoes," you get millions of results. If you filter for "Size 8 AND running AND blue," you get exactly what you need.
  • The Peer-Review Process: Compare peer review to a rigorous quality control inspection at a car factory. Peer-reviewed articles are checked by experts before publication to ensure they are safe and accurate to use.
  • Accessible Research (WCAG 2.1 AA): Explain that finding information is only half the battle; presenting it accessibly is the other half. Compare Alt-text to a radio broadcaster calling a sports game for listeners who cannot see the screen.

3. Practice Activity 1: The CRAAP Test (15 Minutes)

Activity: Team Project & Group Evaluation

  • Action: Divide students into small groups. Give each group two printed (and digitally accessible) articles on the same topic: one scholarly journal and one popular media source.
  • Task: Evaluate both sources using the CRAAP test handout.
  • UDL & Accessibility: Provide articles in large-print text and email a screen-reader compatible PDF version right before the activity.

4. Practice Activity 2: Keyboard Navigation (10 Minutes)

Activity: Interactive Learning Lab

  • Action: Instruct students to unplug their mouse or disable their trackpad and navigate the library database using only their keyboard (Tab, Enter, Arrows).
  • Purpose: Builds empathy and demonstrates why keyboard-only navigation is a vital accessibility requirement.
Accommodation Flag: Students with motor control differences may find keyboard-only navigation physically fatiguing. Alternative: Have students pair up; one verbalizes the navigation steps while the partner executes the keystrokes.

5. Check for Understanding & Closing (10 Minutes)

  • Think-Pair-Share: Discuss the biggest barrier faced when navigating without a mouse and how it changes the way they will format their own assignments.
  • One-Minute Paper: Write down one new search strategy learned and one reason why making digital research accessible matters.

Student Survival Guide Handout

Note to Facilitator: Ensure this document is distributed as a tagged PDF or nicely formatted Word document with high color contrast.

Key Terms to Know

  • CRAAP Test: A checklist to help decide if a source is trustworthy: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose.
  • Scholarly Journals: Magazines for experts that go through strict "quality control" (peer review) before publication.
  • Citations: Giving credit to the original author. It’s like providing a map to the exact books or articles you used.
  • Alternative Text (Alt-text): A hidden written description of an image. If a student cannot see the image, their computer reads it out loud.
  • Screen Reader Compatibility: Designing documents so software used by blind or low-vision users can read the content in a logical order.

Boolean Operators: Your Search Filters

  • AND: Narrows your search (e.g., Climate Change AND Ocean)
  • OR: Broadens your search (e.g., Teenagers OR Adolescents)
  • NOT: Excludes words (e.g., Apple NOT Computers)

Formative Assessment

1. Which Boolean operator would you use if you want to find articles that include BOTH the words "College" and "Stress"?

A) OR
B) AND
C) NOT

2. True or False: If an automated accessibility checker says your document has no errors, it is 100% accessible to someone using a screen reader.

Answer Key:
1. B (AND).
2. False. Automated tools cannot tell if your alt-text is actually descriptive, or if your reading order makes sense. Human testing is always needed.

Lesson 1: Database Search Strategies

Finding the right academic research is completely different from a standard Google search. To find scholarly journals, you need to use Library Databases and Boolean Operators (AND, OR, NOT).

Real-World Example: Think of Boolean operators like online shopping filters. If you search an online store for "shoes," you get millions of results. But if you apply filters for "Size 8 AND running AND blue," you get exactly what you need. Library databases work exactly the same way!

Key Operators to Know

  • AND: Narrows your search by ensuring both words are present (e.g., Climate Change AND Ocean).
  • OR: Broadens your search by allowing either word (e.g., Teenagers OR Adolescents).
  • NOT: Excludes specific words (e.g., Apple NOT Computers).

Lesson 2: Evaluating Sources

Not all information is created equal. Before you use a source in your college papers, you must evaluate its credibility. We use the CRAAP Test to ensure the information is trustworthy.

The CRAAP Test Breakdown

  • Currency: When was the information published? Is it out of date for your topic?
  • Relevance: Does this information directly relate to your research question?
  • Authority: Who is the author or publisher? What are their credentials?
  • Accuracy: Is the information supported by evidence? Has it been peer-reviewed?
  • Purpose: Why does this information exist? Is it to inform, teach, sell, or persuade?
Real-World Example: The peer-review process is like a rigorous quality control inspection at a car factory. You wouldn’t want to drive a car that wasn't tested by expert mechanics. Similarly, peer-reviewed academic articles are checked by subject experts before they are published to ensure the information is safe, accurate, and ready for you to use.

Interactive Lab: The Keyboard Challenge

Accessibility means ensuring digital content is usable by everyone, including those who cannot use a mouse. Automated accessibility tools cannot tell if a website's reading order makes sense or if dropdown menus work without a mouse [12-15].

Your Task

  1. Unplug your mouse or disable your trackpad.
  2. Open your college's library database search page in a new tab.
  3. Try to search for the term "Renewable Energy" using only the Tab, Arrow, and Enter keys on your keyboard.
  4. Reflect: Were you able to access all the search filters? Did you get stuck anywhere?

Note: If you have motor control differences and find keyboard-only navigation fatiguing, you can complete this activity with a partner by verbalizing the navigation steps while they execute the keystrokes.

Test your understanding of the concepts covered in these lessons.

2. If an automated accessibility checker says your document has no errors, it is 100% accessible to someone using a screen reader.
