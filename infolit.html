Lesson 1

Smarter Searching with Boolean Operators

Searching a library database is different from searching Google. Databases respond best to keywords connected by Boolean operators: AND, OR, and NOT. Master these three words and you control exactly what the database brings back.

Real-world example: Boolean operators work like the filters on a shopping website. Search "shoes" and you get millions of results. Filter for "size 8 AND running AND blue" and you get exactly what you need. Library databases work the same way.
  • AND narrows your search — both words must appear. college AND stress
  • OR broadens your search — either word can appear. teenagers OR adolescents
  • NOT excludes a word. apple NOT computers

Lesson 2

Evaluating Sources with the CRAAP Test

Not all information deserves a spot in your paper. Before you cite a source, run it through the CRAAP Test:

  • C — Currency: When was it published? Is that recent enough for your topic?
  • R — Relevance: Does it actually answer your research question?
  • A — Authority: Who wrote it, and what are their credentials?
  • A — Accuracy: Is it supported by evidence? Was it peer-reviewed?
  • P — Purpose: Does it exist to inform — or to sell, entertain, or persuade?
Real-world example: Peer review is like quality control at a car factory. You wouldn't drive a car no mechanic ever inspected. Peer-reviewed articles are checked by subject experts before publication so you know they're road-ready for your research.

Lesson 3

AI and Information: Verify Before You Trust

AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, and Gemini can explain concepts, brainstorm, and summarize — but they can also produce information that sounds confident and is completely wrong, including made-up citations. Researchers call these errors hallucinations.

Real-world example: Treat an AI chatbot like a brilliant friend with an unreliable memory. Great for ideas and explanations — but you'd never repeat their claims in front of your professor without checking the facts yourself first.

The three-step AI verification habit

  1. Trace it: Ask where the claim comes from. If the AI gives a citation, find the original source in the library database. If it doesn't exist, don't use it.
  2. Cross-check it: Confirm key facts in at least one independent, credible source.
  3. Disclose it: Follow your instructor's policy on AI use, and cite AI assistance when your class requires it.
Campus tip: Every TCC course can set its own AI policy. When in doubt, check the syllabus or ask your instructor before using AI on an assignment — and the Learning Commons can help you verify anything an AI tells you.

Hands-On Lab

Accessibility Lab: The Keyboard Challenge

Accessible information matters as much as accurate information. Many people navigate the web entirely by keyboard — no mouse. This quick lab builds that empathy and shows you why document structure matters in your own assignments.

  1. Set your mouse or trackpad aside.
  2. Open the TCC library database page in a new tab.
  3. Search for "renewable energy" using only Tab, Shift+Tab, arrow keys, and Enter.
  4. Notice: could you reach every filter? Did you ever get stuck?
Accessible alternative: If keyboard-only navigation is fatiguing or not workable for you, pair up — one partner calls out the navigation steps while the other presses the keys, then switch roles.
