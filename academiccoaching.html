Lesson 1

Become the Author of Your Own Story

Developmental psychologist Robert Kegan describes a powerful shift that happens as adults grow: moving from a socialized mind — where your choices are steered mostly by what others expect — toward a self-authoring mind, where you weigh those expectations against your own values and write your own script.

In college, this shift shows up everywhere. Did you pick your major, your schedule, your study habits — or did they get picked for you?

Real-world example: Think of your college journey like a video game. A socialized mind plays as an NPC — following whatever quest someone else assigns. A self-authoring mind plays as the main character: still listening to advice from the party, but deciding which quests align with the story they're building.

Lesson 2

Set Goals That Actually Work

"Do better this semester" isn't a goal — it's a wish. Effective goals are SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. The difference between a wish and a SMART goal is the difference between hoping and planning.

  • S — Specific: What exactly will you do?
  • M — Measurable: How will you know you did it?
  • A — Achievable: Is it realistic given your life right now?
  • R — Relevant: Does it connect to the story you're authoring?
  • T — Time-bound: By when?
Real-world example: A SMART goal is like a GPS destination. "Drive somewhere nice" gets you nowhere — the GPS needs an exact address, a route, and an arrival time. "Attend two tutoring sessions per week until my October exam" is an address your brain can navigate to.

Lesson 3

Own Your 168

Everyone — every honor student, every struggling student, every CEO — gets exactly 168 hours each week. Time management isn't about finding more time; it's about seeing honestly where yours goes and assigning it on purpose.

A reliable rule of thumb for college: plan 2 to 3 hours of study per week for every credit hour. A 12-credit load means 24 to 36 study hours — a part-time job's worth.

Real-world example: Your week is a paycheck of 168 hours. Sleep, work, and class are your "bills" — fixed expenses that come out first. What's left is your discretionary income. Budget it on purpose, or it disappears on impulse purchases like four-hour scroll sessions.

Technique to try: Work in focused sprints — for example, 25 minutes of distraction-free study followed by a 5-minute break, a pattern popularized by the Pomodoro Technique (Cirillo, 2018). Four sprints equal a solid two-hour study block.

Lesson 4

Train a Growth Mindset

Psychologist Carol Dweck's research distinguishes a fixed mindset — believing ability is a trait you either have or don't — from a growth mindset — understanding that ability develops through effort, strategy, and feedback (Dweck, 2006). Students who treat struggle as information, not verdict, persist longer and learn more.

Real-world example: Nobody walks into a gym, fails to lift 300 pounds, and concludes "I'm not a weights person." They start lighter and build. Your brain works the same way — a hard calculus problem is a heavy rep, not a verdict on who you are.
Try it: Reframe the Thought

For each thought, decide: fixed or growth?

"I failed the quiz — I'm just not a math person."

"That essay grade stung. I'm booking a writing tutor to figure out what to change for the next one."

"Everyone else codes faster than me, so I should probably drop the class."

The magic word: Add "yet" to fixed thoughts. "I can't do this" becomes "I can't do this yet" — a sentence with a future in it.
